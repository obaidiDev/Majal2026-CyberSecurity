Day 2 · The First Foothold

Every breach starts with
one way in.

Yesterday you learned what a system is supposed to protect. Today you switch sides: you are the attacker. Before you can touch anything inside, you have to find a target, learn it, and get through the door — without ever "hacking" in the movie sense.

Set the role-play: for the next three days they are the attacker. Today is only the entry — pick a target, study it, get the first foothold. We stop the moment we're inside; what happens next is Day 3.

The road to a foothold

You don't start inside — you earn your way in

Today's job: gather what's public (recon), turn it into a way in — a lure, a weak password, an exposed service — and land the first foothold. The attacker's real advantage isn't a tool, it's information: almost everything they need, you left in public.
Walk the strip left to right — the four teal stages are today, the faded one is Day 3. Imagery: magnifier-on-a-book (recon), a tackle box of lures (weaponise — note the phishing pun), an air-mail envelope (deliver), a door lock (foothold), a corridor (move through). Land the through-line: recon feeds delivery — the more they know, the better the lure. Sets up why OSINT matters before we phish. (Stage photos: CC0 / public-domain via Openverse, petrol-duotoned.)

Recon · gathering intel

Recon: learn the target before you touch it

Passive no contact

Read what's already public. Invisible & legal.

Google, socials, breach dumps, WHOIS, job posts.

Active you touch it

Probe their systems directly. Richer — but logged.

Port scans, banner grabbing, probing logins.
OSINT = intel from public sources. Passive is legal and unseen; active scanning without permission isn't. Both tools next are passive.
Ground the next two tools. Passive never touches the target (unseen, legal); active does (logged, needs permission). The dork builder and footprint slides are both passive OSINT. Land the surprise: the sheer volume of passive intel is the attacker's real edge. Give the fuller examples verbally here.

Recon · Google dorking

Turn Google into a precision recon tool

Everyone can type words into Google; almost no one uses its operators. Build a query live and watch the plain-English line explain exactly what each operator narrows. Then run the missions FOR REAL on their own devices (they're online): find a recruiter at a company they want, find open jobs, price-hunt something they want — then the attacker's version: exposed files that were left public by accident. Land two things: (1) operators turn a vague search into a scalpel, and (2) an enormous amount leaks into Google by accident — which is exactly why the next step, OSINT on yourself, matters. Ethics: finding public pages is legal; opening data that isn't yours is not.

Recon · turn it on yourself

Now OSINT the easiest target you have: you

The dork skills they just learned point at a company — now aim them at themselves. Everything is offline and private: what they type never leaves the browser (say this out loud — real email/phone). Have them fill their own identifiers and watch the exposure cards light up, then actually run the "Check live →" links on their own devices (they're online): Have I Been Pwned for the email, a username sweep, Google dorks for name/phone. The payoff is the cascade banner (email + reused handle): one breach becomes total account takeover — the danger is the LINKS between leaks, not any single one. Close on the empowering half: every card has a "shrink it" fix. This is the defender's mindset sneaking into the attacker's week.

Weaponise · build the payload

Weaponise: turn intel into something that acts

Credential harvester

A fake login page. They “sign in” — you get the password.

The #1 phishing payload.

Malicious attachment

A doc, installer, or ISO that runs code the moment it's opened.

Macro doc · fake setup · .lnk

Malicious link

One click to an exploit page — or to the harvester above.

Often hidden behind a short URL.

Malicious QR quishing

A QR hides the URL, so no one checks where it really goes.

Posters, fake parking meters, emails.
The best weapon is believable and evasive — it looks normal and slips filters. That's exactly what you'll tune in the lure, next.
The missing kill-chain stage: weaponise = packaging intel into a payload the target will trust and run. Four families — a credential harvester (fake login, the most common), a malicious attachment (runs code), a malicious link, and a malicious QR (quishing). Don't dwell; the point is what the lure CARRIES. It sets up the believable-vs-evasive tension they'll drive in the phishing lab two slides on.

Deliver · the human exploit

Social engineering: hack the person, not the machine

Social engineering = manipulating people into breaking security — handing over information, clicking a link, or granting access. No firewall stops it, because it targets trust. Every lure below pulls one or more of these five levers.

Authority

We obey people who seem in charge.

“This is IT — reset your password now.”

Urgency

A deadline stops us thinking.

“Your account closes in 24 hours.”

Scarcity

Fear of missing out overrides caution.

“Only 3 offer slots left.”

Familiarity

We trust the known — a brand, a colleague.

“Hi, it's Sara from Finance…”

Fear

Panic makes us act, then verify.

“Suspicious login — secure your account.”
Phishing is social engineering at scale. When you build a lure in two slides' time, notice which levers you're pulling — and why they work on all of us.
The umbrella concept before phishing. Land: social engineering targets trust, not tech — which is why it beats every firewall. Walk the five levers fast; they'll recognise all of them from real messages. Point forward: phishing = these levers delivered by message, at scale. Ask the room which lever fooled them last.

Deliver · phishing

Phishing: one con, many delivery channels

Definition first: phishing is a fraudulent message impersonating someone you trust to make you click, log in, or pay. Then the taxonomy — click each type to see a real-looking example with its red flags called out. Land the axes: it's the same con delivered by email / SMS / voice, and it scales from bulk (spray-and-pray) to targeted (spear, whaling, BEC — where the OSINT from earlier pays off). These examples set up the lure they build next.

Deliver · social engineering

Build the lure: believable enough to click, quiet enough to slip filters

Hand them the attacker's console. Have them toggle the cheap tricks first (urgency, look-alike domain, masked link) — watch click-likelihood rise but the red-flag score rise with it, and the defender panel name each tell. Then toggle Personalise (OSINT) alone: click-likelihood jumps, red-flag score barely moves. That's the lesson — the detail that makes a lure land came from the recon they'll do earlier in the day. Hover the button to expose the real link (masked-link tell). Land it: this is why spear-phishing beats spray-and-pray, and why your public footprint is the ammunition.

The landscape before the console. These are the real initial-access vectors (they map to MITRE ATT&CK's Initial Access tactic — mention, don't deep-dive). The through-line to land: none of this is movie hacking; it's logins and unpatched boxes. The next slide lets them drive three of these and watch defences block each one.

Foothold · initial access

Land the first shell — they log in, they don't “hack”

The payoff of the whole day. Target and loot carry over: the exposed services came from recon, the reused password came from the breach cascade in the footprint slide. Run each method with all defences OFF and watch a real shell land — no exploit-movie, just a login or an unpatched box (that's the reality behind most breaches). Then flip defences ON: MFA and a unique password kill both credential paths; a patch or EDR kills the CVE path. Turn everything on → every door here dies. Two things to land: (1) initial access is mostly logins and known holes, not movie hacking; (2) each specific control closes a specific door — the seed of Day 5's defence. Stop at the shell: moving and escalating is Day 3.
